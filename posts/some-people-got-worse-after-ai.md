三個月前，我開始認真在 MantaGO 裡面推動 AI 工具的導入。我做了很多事——制定了使用規格、調整了 code review 標準、在 1on1 裡面加了「不看 AI 說你的邏輯」的問題、搞了「判斷軌跡」的回顧機制。

我以為我做得夠多了。

上週五我拉了一份三個月的回顧數據，才發現我漏掉了一個我不太敢面對的事實：**有兩個原本表現穩定的人，在 AI 導入之後，明顯退步了。**

不是退步在產出量——產出量都上升了。退步在思考的品質。

---

## 退步不長得像退步

這是最陰險的地方。

如果一個人的產出量下降、deadline miss、品質變差，你很快就會介入。但這兩個人的問題不是這些——他們交出來的東西看起來很好，甚至比以前更好。文件更完整，分析更全面，code 結構更乾淨。

問題出在一個更微妙的地方：他們不再犯錯了。

這聽起來像是稱讚，但不是。

這兩個人以前犯的錯，很多時候是因為他們嘗試了更大膽的方向，或者挑戰了既有做法。他們的錯誤讓我知道他們在思考。有時候想錯了，但那個「真的有在想」的軌跡在。

現在他們不再犯那種錯。交出來的東西安全、正確、完整——但也可預測、不意外、沒有「等等，我沒想過這個角度」的瞬間。

他們從「犯有趣的錯的人」變成了「交出沒人能挑剔的東西的人」。

AI 幫了他們——幫他們消除了冒險的理由。

---

## 我自己的問題：我建的機制可能在懲罰思考

這讓我非常不舒服，因為我開始懷疑，我自己推動的機制有副作用。

「不看 AI 說你的邏輯」——這個問題的初衷是驗證人真的有在思考。但我漏看了一件事：對某些人來說，這個問題聽起來像考試。而考試讓人趨向保守。

如果你知道我會問「你為什麼做這個決定」，而你不確定自己的邏輯夠不夠強，你會怎麼做？你會選一個更安全的決定。一個 AI 已經驗證過的決定。一個有數據支持的決定。一個不太會被挑戰的決定。

然後我問的時候，你給出一個打磨過的答案。

但那個答案可能不是最好的決定——它只是最不會被質疑的決定。

我的機制，意外地在獎勵防守，而不是思考。

---

## 新創最不能失去的東西

這件事如果只是管理問題，我睡一覺可能就想通了。讓我睡不著的是它背後的產品含義。

MantaGO 是一個早期產品。早期產品最需要的，不是不犯錯——是犯對的錯。你需要有人敢說「我覺得用戶不是這樣想的，雖然數據看起來是」。你需要有人敢做一個 AI 不會建議你做的功能，因為它來自一個只有人才能感受到的直覺。

如果我的團隊在 AI 的輔助下變得越來越「正確」，但也越來越不敢賭——我們就失去了新創最核心的能力：在不確定性裡面做出不確定的決定，然後用速度去驗證。

不確定性不是 bug，是 feature。AI 很擅長消除不確定性，但有些不確定性需要被保留，因為那是創新住的地方。

我回想起一個月前，我們決定讓 AI 生成的行程有「個性」——敢推薦評分不高但有味道的地方、敢用口語化的語言。沒有任何 AI 分析會建議你做這個決定。它來自對用戶的理解，以及願意拿自己的判斷去賭。

如果我團隊裡的人不再做這種決定，MantaGO 就完了——不是被 AI 殺死的，是被我建立的框架殺死的。

---

## 我做的修正

上禮拜，我改了 1on1 裡面問的問題。

之前：「不看 AI 說你的邏輯？」

現在：「這個決定裡面，你最不確定的部分是什麼？」

改動看起來很小。心理效果完全不同。

第一個問題考的是你有沒有想清楚。第二個問題邀請你承認你不知道什麼。

第一個問題獎勵確定性——你的邏輯越清楚，我越滿意。第二個問題獎勵誠實——你越能辨識自己的不確定性，我越相信你真的有在想。

因為真的深入思考，不會讓你更確定，而是讓你更清楚自己不確定什麼。

我試了一個禮拜，結果很有意思：那兩個「退步」的人突然有很多話想說。他們不是沒在想。他們一直帶著懷疑和不確定——只是在舊的框架下，覺得這些東西不應該被說出來。

---

## 判斷軌跡 2.0：追蹤懷疑，不只是決定

我也修改了「判斷軌跡」的回顧格式。

舊版問的是：「AI 建議了什麼 → 你選了什麼 → 你在哪裡跟 AI 不同？」

新版多了兩個欄位：

- **這個決定裡面，你最沒信心的假設是什麼？**
- **如果三個月後這個決定是錯的，最可能的原因是什麼？**

這兩個問題做了一件事：**把「我不知道」從一種弱點，變成一種能力。**

知道自己不知道什麼，是判斷力裡面最進階的部分。AI 不會告訴你它不知道什麼（至少在大部分情況下不會誠實地告訴你），所以你不能把這件事外包出去。這是人唯一無法委託的認知能力。

---

## 技術債和管理債的相似性

工程師都懂技術債：你為了速度跳過的東西，遲早會回來找你。

我發現管理也有一模一樣的東西。我叫它「管理債」。

我建立的那些 AI 使用規範——使用規格、review 標準、判斷軌跡——每一個單獨看都是合理的。但疊在一起，它們形成了一個隱性的訊息：**正確比冒險重要。**

這就是管理債。我為了解決「判斷力退化」這個問題，建了一堆框架，但這些框架的副作用是壓抑了冒險的意願。然後我花了三個月才發現。

技術債有 linter、有 SonarQube、有自動化工具可以偵測。管理債沒有。唯一的偵測器是你願不願意看那些你不太想看到的信號——比如兩個不再犯有趣錯誤的人。

---

## 我現在相信的事

三個月前，我覺得 AI 導入最大的風險是判斷力退化。

現在我覺得最大的風險不是退化——是**馴化**。

退化是你的思考能力變弱了。馴化是你的思考能力還在，但你選擇不用——因為環境在告訴你：安全比正確重要，被認可比做好決定重要。

AI 不會馴化你。但你建立的制度、你問的問題、你獎勵的行為——這些會。

制度是我建的。所以這件事是我的責任。

好消息是：制度可以重建。我已經開始了。

壞消息是：我也不確定重建的方向是對的。

但「不確定」——我剛學到，這是一種能力，不是弱點。

所以我繼續往前，帶著不確定。

Three months ago, I started seriously pushing AI tool adoption at MantaGO. I did a lot — wrote usage specs, adjusted code review standards, added "tell me your logic without looking at AI" to my 1-on-1s, built a "judgment trace" retrospective system.

I thought I'd done enough.

Last Friday, I pulled a three-month review and discovered something I wasn't ready to face: **two people who had been performing steadily got noticeably worse after AI adoption.**

Not worse in output — output went up across the board. Worse in the quality of their thinking.

---

## Decline Doesn't Look Like Decline

This is the insidious part.

When someone's output drops, misses deadlines, or quality tanks, you notice fast. But these two didn't have any of those symptoms. Their deliverables looked good — better, even. Documents were more complete. Analysis was more thorough. Code was structurally cleaner.

The problem showed up somewhere subtler: they stopped making mistakes.

That sounds like a compliment. It isn't.

The mistakes these two used to make were often the result of trying a bolder direction or challenging an established approach. Their mistakes told me they were thinking. Sometimes they thought wrong, but the trace of genuine thinking was there.

Now they don't make those kinds of mistakes. What they deliver is safe, correct, complete — and also predictable, unsurprising, with no moments of "wait, I hadn't considered that angle."

They went from "people who make interesting mistakes" to "people who always deliver things no one can criticize."

AI helped them. It helped them eliminate the reason to take risks.

---

## My Own Problem: The Mechanisms I Built Might Be Punishing Thinking

This made me deeply uncomfortable, because I started to suspect that the mechanisms I'd championed had side effects.

"Tell me your logic without looking at AI" — the intent was to verify that people were actually thinking. But I missed something: for certain people, that question sounds like an exam. And exams make people risk-averse.

If you know I'm going to ask "why did you make this decision," and you're not confident your reasoning is strong enough, what do you do? You pick a safer decision. One that AI has validated. One backed by data. One that's unlikely to be challenged.

Then when I ask, you give a polished answer.

But that answer might not be the best decision — it's just the least questionable one.

My mechanism was accidentally rewarding defense, not thinking.

---

## The Thing a Startup Can't Afford to Lose

If this were just a management problem, I could sleep on it and figure it out. What kept me up was the product implication.

MantaGO is an early-stage product. Early-stage products don't need to avoid mistakes — they need to make the *right* mistakes. You need people willing to say "I think users don't think this way, even though the data suggests otherwise." You need people willing to build a feature AI would never recommend, because it comes from an intuition only a human can feel.

If my team becomes increasingly "correct" under AI's guidance but increasingly unwilling to bet — we lose the core capability of a startup: making uncertain decisions in uncertain conditions, then using speed to validate.

Uncertainty isn't a bug. It's a feature. AI is excellent at eliminating uncertainty, but some uncertainty needs to be preserved, because that's where innovation lives.

I think back to a month ago: we decided to give AI-generated itineraries "personality" — daring to recommend places with low ratings but real character, daring to use casual language. No AI analysis would have recommended that decision. It came from knowing what users wanted, and being willing to bet my judgment on it.

If the people on my team stop making decisions like that, MantaGO is finished — not killed by AI, but killed by the framework I built around it.

---

## A Correction I Made

Last week, I changed the question I ask in 1-on-1s.

Before: "Can you tell me your logic without looking at AI?"

Now: "What's the part of this decision you're least certain about?"

The change looks small. The psychological effect is completely different.

The first question tests whether you've thought things through. The second question invites you to admit what you don't know.

The first question rewards certainty — the clearer your logic, the more satisfied I am. The second question rewards honesty — the better you can identify your own uncertainties, the more I believe you're actually thinking.

Because thinking with real depth doesn't make you more certain. It makes you clearer about what you're uncertain about.

I tried it for a week. The results were fascinating: those two "declining" people suddenly had a lot to say. They weren't not-thinking. They'd been carrying doubts and uncertainties the whole time — they just felt, under the old framework, that those things shouldn't be voiced.

---

## Judgment Traces 2.0: Tracking Doubt, Not Just Decisions

I also revised the "judgment trace" retrospective format.

The old version asked: "What did AI recommend → What did you choose → Where did you diverge from AI?"

The new version adds two fields:

- **What's the assumption in this decision you're least confident about?**
- **If this decision turns out to be wrong in three months, what's the most likely reason?**

These two questions do one thing: **they turn "I don't know" from a weakness into a capability.**

Knowing what you don't know is the most advanced component of judgment. AI won't tell you what it doesn't know (or at least, it won't tell you honestly in most cases), so you can't outsource this. It's the one cognitive skill that's genuinely non-delegatable.

---

## Technical Debt and Management Debt

Engineers understand technical debt: the things you skip for speed will come back to find you.

I've realized management has an identical phenomenon. I'm calling it "management debt."

The AI guidelines I built — usage specs, review standards, judgment traces — each one is individually reasonable. But stacked together, they sent an implicit message: **correctness matters more than risk-taking.**

That's management debt. I built a bunch of frameworks to solve "judgment atrophy," but the side effect of those frameworks was suppressing the willingness to take risks. And it took me three months to notice.

Technical debt has linters, SonarQube, automated tools to catch it. Management debt has none of that. The only detector is your willingness to look at signals you'd rather not see — like two people who stopped making interesting mistakes.

---

## What I Believe Now

Three months ago, I thought the biggest risk of AI adoption was judgment atrophy.

Now I think the biggest risk isn't atrophy — it's **domestication**.

Atrophy means your thinking ability weakens. Domestication means your thinking ability is intact, but you choose not to use it — because the environment is telling you: safety matters more than correctness, being approved matters more than making a good call.

AI doesn't domesticate you. But the systems you build, the questions you ask, the behaviors you reward — those can.

I'm the one who built the systems. So this one's on me.

The good news: systems can be rebuilt. I've already started.

The bad news: I'm not sure the rebuild is right either.

But "not sure" — I just learned that's a capability, not a weakness.

So I'm moving forward, uncertain.
